Research

Construction of 3D genome

Our first goal is to construct a 3D configuration of a chromatin segment using simulation techniques. Recent experimental data (from chromosome conformation capture, 3C/Hi-C/Micro-C) allow us to get the contact frequency map at a resolution as low as 200bp (nucleosome level). We convert this contact frequency map to a contact probability matrix and an interaction energy matrix between all the loci.

Using molecular dynamics package LAMMPS, we simulate a bead-spring polymer via Langevin dynamics taking constructed interaction matrix as an input for inter-loci LJ interaction strength. Once equilibrated, we extract an ensemble of uncorrelated polymer configurations in 3D. We analyze the 3D structures and use them in various cases.

Presently, we put attractive LJ potential between the beads with a cut-off at 1.5\sigma. Hence, the polymer forms small clusters, but the current strategy fails to form long-range contacts. We are planning to introduce new interaction acting, especially between far-away beads. We expect this modification will help us to capture contact frequencies at all ranges.

Dynamics of transcription factors

Transcription factors (TF) are proteins that bind to a target site on chromatin and orchestrate transcription. We try to model the interphase chromatin as a network where each bead of the simulated polymer acts as a node and the TF as an unbiased random walker, hopping to other nodes in the neighborhood. From existing literature on the network, it is known that the mean first passage time (MFPT) of a random walker between any two nodes on a general network follows a set of particular scaling laws.

  • Our main goal is to verify whether the MFPT scaling law (for a network) holds in the chromatin context, i.e., the possibility of characterizing the MFPT of a TF based on walk dimension and fractal dimension of chromatin mesh.
  • The biologically relevant question is, if a TF is stuck inside a wrong TAD (chromatin mesh with no target site), how long it would take to come out of it. In other words, we are trying to figure out the dependence of mean exit time from a TAD on the size of the domain (radius of gyration) and structure of this domain.
  • It is now well accepted that chromatin are dynamic polymers. This dynamic nature arises due to the tethering of chromatin to the nuclear lamina, the interplay of interactions with various proteins, and the dynamic loop extrusion process via cohesin.Previous works show that the performance of a random walker (walking on polymer) is increased if the polymer has its dynamics. The mean square displacement of a random walker along a static polymer shows only diffusive behavior, whereas polymer dynamics can make the walk superdiffusive. It will be interesting to see whether chromatin dynamics can reduce the TF's mean target search time (or mean exit time from a TAD).

  • Effect of collapsed structure: Chromatin is a collapsed polymer and it makes frequent contact with itself. TF can jump to distant loci if there are contacts between far-away loci. We constructed an artificial domain where each locus is making contacts with other loci with a constant probability. Mean exit time from the domain for a random walker shows a non-monotonic behavior. If the contact probability is small, the walk is only along the 1D chain and can not hop to distant loci. Again, if contact probability is very high, there are too many contacts for each locus and the walker takes more time to reach the domain boundary.
  • Exit time analysis: We constructed an ensemble of connectivity matrices (Cij) (with entries 1 and 0) for a region on human chromosome 7 according to the available Hi-C contact matrix. A random walker was allowed to walk on the network following the connectivity matrix (i.e. only if Cij=1, jump is allowed between i and j ). The chosen region consists a TAD and we calculated exit time distribution. Simulation have shown that total mean exit time from a bigger domain is less than sum of mean exit times from the sub-domains.
  • Exit time scaling: We collected more TAD structures from the Hi-C map in order to find out a proper scaling of mean exit times with TAD lengths.
  • Finite-size of the TAD: As discussed earlier, Our goal is to calculate mean exit time scaling with the size of the domain. However, the actual TAD size varies in the range 0.5Mbp to 2Mbp. Hence, getting a scaling law remains a challenge with the available data.

    • Recombination

    One of the major reasons for the diversity of living beings is the biological process of recombination—two parts of the broken DNA meet together and exchange. This phenomenon can give rise to entirely new species during evolution. What is the rate of recombination has been an important question historically. Since chromatin is a polymer and recombination requires the physical proximity of two chromatin segments, this question cannot be entirely answered until we incorporate the chromatin 3D organization into our calculations. Recent advances in experimental techniques have led to new chromatin conformation capture experiments (3C, Hi-C). It provides us the probability that two chromatin segments are in contact. We plan to use this and predict the recombination frequency under various conditions.

    Our questions: what is the probability that two loci (with the same sequence identity) from two different chromatin come into the vicinity and recombine:

  • If chromatin is modeled as a random walk polymer.
  • If chromatin is modeled as a self-avoiding walk (SAW) polymer.
  • If chromatin is modeled as a compact polymer with similar folding as seen in experimental data.
    • The first two cases are control scenarios that allow us to analyze the effect of the specific organization of chromatin on the recombination process. Moreover, searching for another exact copy of chromatin segment requires extensive scanning. However, the process takes only tens of minutes to a few hours. To explain the surprisingly fast time-scale of recombination, one may need to introduce new interactions. Also, dependence on the length of the chromosome can be interesting to study.

    Chromatin organisation at different stage of cell cycle

    The interphase stage of the cell cycle is divided into three parts: G1, S and G2. The organization of chromatin is very different in these stages. Recently, we have the experimental data for one of the species of Yeast (in the form of Hi-C), which also shows variability in the organization across different stages of the cell cycle.

  • We want to model interphase chromatin at different points of interphase using our technique discussed earlier and we want to see whether our polymer model can capture the essential features of organizations.
  • We also want to compare the volume and shape of chromatin with the results from experiments.
  • Another aspect of this project is to predict a possible pathway through which chromatin reaches G1 to G2 during a cell cycle.
